# Community Characteristics Framework

## Foundation for the Simba Community Development Engine

### Purpose

This document defines the developmental characteristics that strengthen a healthy, self-sufficient community. These characteristics are **not personality traits**, fixed identities, or permanent labels. They are developable qualities that any member can strengthen through reflection, learning, practice, mentorship, and real-world contribution.

This is a planning and architecture document only. It does **not** implement scoring, code, assessment changes, or production data models.

---

## Relationship to Existing Architecture

Garvey remains the **Observation Engine**.

Garvey measures and stores evidence about:

- Behaviors
- Preferences
- Patterns
- Assessment archetypes
- Supporting evidence

Simba remains the **Interpretation Engine**.

Simba will eventually interpret Garvey evidence to determine:

- Community Archetype alignment
- Growth pathways
- Community contribution opportunities
- Community Characteristics development recommendations

Garvey never assigns Community Characteristics. Simba may interpret evidence from Garvey, but the interpretation should always be presented as current development, not permanent identity.

---

## Guiding Principle

Every Community Characteristic must satisfy three requirements:

1. It strengthens the individual.
2. It strengthens the community.
3. It can be intentionally developed through learning and practice.

No characteristic should permanently define a member. Members should always be able to grow, change direction, deepen their strengths, and build new capacities.

---

## Language Philosophy

The system should avoid fixed labels and use growth-oriented language.

Preferred language:

- "You currently demonstrate strong evidence of cooperation."
- "Your recent activity suggests growth in reliability."
- "Here are ways to strengthen financial stewardship."
- "This community opportunity may help you practice leadership."

Avoid language such as:

- "You are a leader."
- "You are not disciplined."
- "You are permanently low in accountability."
- "This is your fixed community role."

Community Archetypes describe **where** a member may naturally contribute. Community Characteristics describe **how** they contribute and what they can develop over time.

## Deliverable 2: Characteristic Definitions and Profiles

Each profile includes definition, community importance, observable behaviors, possible Garvey evidence, related archetypes, strengthening practices, learning resources, and community activities.

### Cooperation

**Definition:** The ability to work constructively with others toward shared goals while respecting different roles, skills, and perspectives.

**Why it matters:** Cooperative communities require members who can coordinate action, share credit, reduce duplication, and build trust across households, teams, and generations.

**Observable behaviors:**

- Participates in group tasks without dominating or withdrawing.
- Shares information that helps others succeed.
- Offers help when another member is blocked.
- Accepts agreed-upon group decisions even when personal preference differs.

**Related Garvey evidence:** Team preference responses, collaborative task completion, participation patterns, peer feedback, event attendance, project contributions.

**Related Community Archetypes:** Organizer, Care Builder, Economic Builder, Cultural Keeper, Educator, Mediator, Infrastructure Steward.

**Ways to strengthen it:** Practice shared planning, rotate roles in group projects, ask clarifying questions, document commitments, and debrief team outcomes.

**Suggested learning resources:** Cooperative economics primers, group facilitation guides, restorative practice introductions, teamwork workshops.

**Suggested community activities:** Mutual aid shifts, neighborhood cleanups, community garden teams, cooperative purchasing groups, study circles.

### Responsibility

**Definition:** The willingness to own duties, commitments, choices, and consequences.

**Why it matters:** Responsibility allows a community to depend on members without excessive supervision or repeated reminders.

**Observable behaviors:**

- Completes assigned duties or communicates early when unable.
- Takes care of personal obligations that affect the group.
- Corrects mistakes without deflecting blame.
- Protects community agreements and shared expectations.

**Related Garvey evidence:** Commitment completion, missed task patterns, reflection entries, role acceptance, follow-up behavior.

**Related Community Archetypes:** Organizer, Governance Steward, Economic Builder, Educator, Infrastructure Steward.

**Ways to strengthen it:** Use personal commitment tracking, define scope before accepting work, practice weekly self-review, and pair with an accountability partner.

**Suggested learning resources:** Time management courses, personal finance basics, civic responsibility readings, project management introductions.

**Suggested community activities:** Volunteer coordinator support, event setup roles, rotating maintenance duties, committee participation.

### Reliability

**Definition:** The ability to follow through consistently, arrive prepared, and be counted on by others.

**Why it matters:** Reliability lowers coordination costs and protects community projects from collapse due to uncertainty.

**Observable behaviors:**

- Arrives on time for agreed activities.
- Completes recurring tasks consistently.
- Gives realistic estimates of availability.
- Maintains communication when circumstances change.

**Related Garvey evidence:** Attendance history, completion streaks, calendar adherence, participation consistency, project delivery timelines.

**Related Community Archetypes:** Infrastructure Steward, Economic Builder, Organizer, Care Builder, Governance Steward.

**Ways to strengthen it:** Start with small recurring commitments, use reminders, confirm expectations in writing, and review capacity before saying yes.

**Suggested learning resources:** Habit formation books, productivity workshops, calendar planning guides, operations checklists.

**Suggested community activities:** Weekly food distribution, recurring childcare support, meeting note-taking, facility opening and closing routines.

### Stewardship

**Definition:** The practice of caring for shared resources, relationships, knowledge, spaces, and systems so they remain useful for future members.

**Why it matters:** Stewardship protects the commons and ensures that community resources are not exhausted, neglected, or privatized without accountability.

**Observable behaviors:**

- Maintains shared spaces and tools.
- Documents knowledge for others.
- Uses resources carefully and transparently.
- Thinks about long-term consequences before acting.

**Related Garvey evidence:** Resource usage logs, maintenance participation, documentation contributions, sustainability preferences, governance participation.

**Related Community Archetypes:** Infrastructure Steward, Cultural Keeper, Economic Builder, Governance Steward, Land Steward.

**Ways to strengthen it:** Learn asset management basics, practice documentation, participate in maintenance planning, and study community land and cooperative models.

**Suggested learning resources:** Commons governance resources, cooperative ownership guides, environmental stewardship courses, archival practice guides.

**Suggested community activities:** Tool library care, community garden maintenance, oral history archiving, cooperative budget review.

### Discipline

**Definition:** The capacity to apply focus, restraint, practice, and preparation in service of meaningful goals.

**Why it matters:** Discipline turns intention into durable community capacity.

**Observable behaviors:**

- Maintains practice routines.
- Prepares before meetings or service commitments.
- Avoids impulsive decisions that harm group work.
- Persists through necessary but difficult tasks.

**Related Garvey evidence:** Learning streaks, preparation behavior, goal completion, reflection consistency, skill-practice logs.

**Related Community Archetypes:** Economic Builder, Educator, Infrastructure Steward, Health Builder, Cultural Keeper.

**Ways to strengthen it:** Set small routines, practice delayed gratification, use progress reviews, and connect habits to community purpose.

**Suggested learning resources:** Habit formation, study skills, health routines, apprenticeship models, martial arts or disciplined arts programs.

**Suggested community activities:** Skill labs, fitness groups, study cohorts, apprenticeship rotations, savings circles.

### Integrity

**Definition:** Acting consistently with truth, values, agreements, and community trust, especially when no one is watching.

**Why it matters:** Integrity is the foundation for safe collaboration, shared resources, economic exchange, and community governance.

**Observable behaviors:**

- Tells the truth even when inconvenient.
- Honors confidentiality and boundaries.
- Keeps agreements or renegotiates transparently.
- Refuses exploitation, manipulation, or misuse of trust.

**Related Garvey evidence:** Peer trust signals, dispute history, agreement adherence, transparency in financial or project activities, reflection patterns.

**Related Community Archetypes:** Governance Steward, Mediator, Economic Builder, Educator, Organizer.

**Ways to strengthen it:** Clarify values, practice transparent communication, review agreements before acting, and seek counsel when conflicts of interest arise.

**Suggested learning resources:** Ethics readings, restorative justice materials, governance training, fiduciary responsibility basics.

**Suggested community activities:** Bylaw review circles, transparent budgeting teams, conflict accountability processes, trust-building workshops.

### Initiative

**Definition:** The willingness to begin useful action, identify needs, and create momentum without waiting for perfect conditions.

**Why it matters:** Initiative helps communities respond to opportunities and problems before they become crises.

**Observable behaviors:**

- Notices unmet needs and proposes next steps.
- Starts small prototypes or pilot efforts.
- Volunteers for useful work without being pressured.
- Brings ideas with practical execution plans.

**Related Garvey evidence:** Project proposals, volunteer patterns, opportunity matching, self-directed learning, early action signals.

**Related Community Archetypes:** Economic Builder, Organizer, Innovator, Cultural Keeper, Youth Builder.

**Ways to strengthen it:** Practice small experiments, keep an opportunity journal, ask "what is the next useful step?", and learn basic project scoping.

**Suggested learning resources:** Entrepreneurship basics, design thinking, community organizing guides, project planning courses.

**Suggested community activities:** Pop-up markets, pilot workshops, mutual aid drives, community needs surveys, youth project labs.

### Communication

**Definition:** The ability to listen, express, clarify, document, and coordinate information in ways that help people act together.

**Why it matters:** Communication prevents confusion, reduces conflict, preserves knowledge, and supports collective action.

**Observable behaviors:**

- Listens before responding.
- Summarizes agreements clearly.
- Uses respectful tone in disagreement.
- Shares timely updates with the right people.

**Related Garvey evidence:** Discussion participation, message responsiveness, reflection quality, facilitation roles, peer feedback.

**Related Community Archetypes:** Organizer, Mediator, Educator, Cultural Keeper, Governance Steward.

**Ways to strengthen it:** Practice active listening, write meeting summaries, ask confirmation questions, and learn facilitation basics.

**Suggested learning resources:** Public speaking, nonviolent communication, writing workshops, meeting facilitation guides.

**Suggested community activities:** Town halls, storytelling circles, meeting facilitation, newsletter teams, oral history interviews.

### Compassion

**Definition:** The ability to recognize human need and respond with care, dignity, and appropriate support.

**Why it matters:** Compassion keeps community systems human-centered and prevents efficiency from replacing care.

**Observable behaviors:**

- Notices when members need support.
- Responds without shaming people.
- Balances care with healthy boundaries.
- Makes room for elders, children, disabled members, and vulnerable households.

**Related Garvey evidence:** Caregiving interests, mutual aid participation, peer support behavior, service preferences, reflection themes.

**Related Community Archetypes:** Care Builder, Health Builder, Educator, Mediator, Organizer.

**Ways to strengthen it:** Practice perspective-taking, learn trauma-informed basics, volunteer in support roles, and reflect on boundaries.

**Suggested learning resources:** Trauma-informed care, community health worker introductions, elder care basics, disability inclusion resources.

**Suggested community activities:** Meal trains, elder check-ins, childcare cooperatives, support circles, wellness outreach.

### Service

**Definition:** Contributing time, skills, labor, or resources for the benefit of others and the community as a whole.

**Why it matters:** Service transforms community from an idea into lived mutual support.

**Observable behaviors:**

- Shows up for community needs.
- Shares skills without always requiring personal gain.
- Participates in mutual aid or volunteer activities.
- Helps maintain events, spaces, and systems.

**Related Garvey evidence:** Volunteer history, contribution logs, event participation, skill sharing, stated service interests.

**Related Community Archetypes:** Care Builder, Organizer, Infrastructure Steward, Educator, Cultural Keeper.

**Ways to strengthen it:** Commit to recurring service, identify personal gifts, rotate between visible and invisible labor, and reflect on impact.

**Suggested learning resources:** Mutual aid guides, servant leadership readings, volunteer management resources, community organizing training.

**Suggested community activities:** Food distribution, tutoring, transportation support, event staffing, repair days.

### Accountability

**Definition:** Being answerable to oneself, peers, agreements, and community standards in a way that supports repair and growth.

**Why it matters:** Accountability allows a community to correct harm, maintain trust, and mature without relying only on punishment or avoidance.

**Observable behaviors:**

- Accepts feedback without immediate defensiveness.
- Follows through on repair commitments.
- Names mistakes clearly.
- Supports fair standards for self and others.

**Related Garvey evidence:** Feedback response patterns, dispute resolution records, commitment repair, reflection entries, governance participation.

**Related Community Archetypes:** Governance Steward, Mediator, Organizer, Economic Builder, Educator.

**Ways to strengthen it:** Use accountability partners, practice repair conversations, document commitments, and learn restorative processes.

**Suggested learning resources:** Restorative justice, conflict mediation, governance basics, reflective journaling prompts.

**Suggested community activities:** Peer accountability circles, restorative conferences, committee reporting, transparent project retrospectives.

### Wisdom

**Definition:** The ability to apply knowledge, experience, values, timing, and context to make sound decisions.

**Why it matters:** Wisdom helps communities avoid repeating mistakes and balance urgency with long-term consequences.

**Observable behaviors:**

- Seeks counsel before major decisions.
- Recognizes patterns from history and experience.
- Balances data with human context.
- Shares lessons without arrogance.

**Related Garvey evidence:** Reflection depth, elder engagement, learning history, decision-making patterns, mentorship behavior.

**Related Community Archetypes:** Elder Council, Educator, Governance Steward, Cultural Keeper, Mediator.

**Ways to strengthen it:** Study history, seek intergenerational dialogue, reflect after action, and compare short-term and long-term outcomes.

**Suggested learning resources:** African and diasporic history, ethical leadership, systems thinking, oral tradition studies.

**Suggested community activities:** Elder interviews, study circles, governance retreats, project retrospectives, community archives.

### Adaptability

**Definition:** The capacity to adjust constructively when conditions, resources, roles, or information change.

**Why it matters:** Adaptability helps communities survive disruption without losing purpose.

**Observable behaviors:**

- Adjusts plans when new information emerges.
- Learns new tools or methods when needed.
- Stays calm during uncertainty.
- Helps others transition through change.

**Related Garvey evidence:** Response to changing tasks, learning agility, role flexibility, resilience reflections, project pivots.

**Related Community Archetypes:** Innovator, Organizer, Economic Builder, Infrastructure Steward, Health Builder.

**Ways to strengthen it:** Practice scenario planning, learn new skills, debrief changes, and build emotional regulation habits.

**Suggested learning resources:** Change management, resilience training, emergency preparedness, systems thinking.

**Suggested community activities:** Emergency drills, project pivots, cross-training, pop-up response teams, technology onboarding.

### Resourcefulness

**Definition:** The ability to solve problems creatively and practically using available assets, relationships, knowledge, and tools.

**Why it matters:** Resourcefulness allows communities to build despite scarcity, delay, or limited institutional support.

**Observable behaviors:**

- Identifies usable assets others overlook.
- Repurposes tools or spaces responsibly.
- Builds partnerships to meet needs.
- Finds low-cost first steps before waiting for perfect funding.

**Related Garvey evidence:** Problem-solving responses, project completion under constraints, network use, opportunity discovery, innovation patterns.

**Related Community Archetypes:** Economic Builder, Innovator, Infrastructure Steward, Organizer, Land Steward.

**Ways to strengthen it:** Map available assets, practice constraint-based projects, learn repair skills, and study cooperative financing.

**Suggested learning resources:** Asset-based community development, repair culture, entrepreneurship, grant basics, maker education.

**Suggested community activities:** Tool libraries, repair cafes, community asset mapping, barter networks, cooperative purchasing.

### Creativity

**Definition:** The ability to generate useful, original, culturally grounded, or imaginative ideas, expressions, and solutions.

**Why it matters:** Creativity helps communities tell their stories, solve problems, build identity, and imagine futures beyond current limits.

**Observable behaviors:**

- Produces art, media, designs, or new approaches.
- Connects ideas across domains.
- Helps make community experiences engaging and memorable.
- Uses culture as a source of innovation.

**Related Garvey evidence:** Creative interests, portfolio activity, media contributions, event design, ideation patterns.

**Related Community Archetypes:** Cultural Keeper, Innovator, Educator, Youth Builder, Economic Builder.

**Ways to strengthen it:** Maintain a creative practice, collaborate across disciplines, study cultural traditions, and prototype ideas publicly.

**Suggested learning resources:** Creative writing, design, music production, visual arts, Afrofuturism, cultural history.

**Suggested community activities:** Murals, storytelling nights, youth arts labs, festival planning, media teams, design sprints.

### Leadership

**Definition:** Helping people move toward shared purpose through vision, service, coordination, courage, and care.

**Why it matters:** Leadership provides direction without requiring domination. Healthy leadership multiplies the capacity of others.

**Observable behaviors:**

- Clarifies purpose and next steps.
- Encourages participation from others.
- Shares credit and develops new leaders.
- Makes difficult decisions transparently.

**Related Garvey evidence:** Facilitation roles, project leadership, peer influence, decision-making patterns, mentorship signals.

**Related Community Archetypes:** Organizer, Governance Steward, Economic Builder, Educator, Cultural Keeper.

**Ways to strengthen it:** Lead small projects, learn facilitation, practice delegation, seek feedback, and mentor emerging contributors.

**Suggested learning resources:** Servant leadership, cooperative governance, public speaking, strategic planning, facilitation.

**Suggested community activities:** Committee leadership, youth team coaching, community assemblies, project launches, training cohorts.

### Humility

**Definition:** Remaining teachable, grounded, respectful, and willing to learn from others regardless of status or expertise.

**Why it matters:** Humility prevents ego from damaging collaboration and keeps learning open across generations and roles.

**Observable behaviors:**

- Asks for help when needed.
- Credits others accurately.
- Receives correction with reflection.
- Learns from elders, peers, and youth.

**Related Garvey evidence:** Feedback response, learning behavior, peer collaboration, mentor engagement, reflection tone.

**Related Community Archetypes:** Educator, Mediator, Governance Steward, Cultural Keeper, Care Builder.

**Ways to strengthen it:** Practice feedback rituals, document lessons learned, rotate out of spotlight roles, and seek intergenerational learning.

**Suggested learning resources:** Reflective practice, spiritual or ethical traditions, mentorship guides, leadership humility readings.

**Suggested community activities:** Learning circles, elder-youth exchanges, peer review sessions, apprenticeship, community gratitude rituals.

### Patience

**Definition:** Sustaining respectful effort through delay, difficulty, disagreement, or slow growth.

**Why it matters:** Community building takes time. Patience protects relationships and prevents short-term frustration from destroying long-term work.

**Observable behaviors:**

- Stays engaged when progress is slow.
- Allows others time to learn.
- De-escalates rushed conflict.
- Balances urgency with care.

**Related Garvey evidence:** Long-term participation, response to setbacks, conflict behavior, mentorship behavior, learning persistence.

**Related Community Archetypes:** Mediator, Educator, Care Builder, Cultural Keeper, Organizer.

**Ways to strengthen it:** Practice long-range planning, mindfulness, mentorship, and reflection on delayed outcomes.

**Suggested learning resources:** Conflict de-escalation, emotional regulation, long-term organizing histories, mindfulness practices.

**Suggested community activities:** Tutoring, restorative circles, elder care, multi-month projects, garden cultivation.

### Consistency

**Definition:** Repeating constructive actions over time so growth, trust, and community capacity can compound.

**Why it matters:** Consistency turns isolated effort into dependable culture.

**Observable behaviors:**

- Maintains helpful routines.
- Shows steady participation over time.
- Builds habits that support shared goals.
- Avoids repeated cycles of overcommitment and disappearance.

**Related Garvey evidence:** Participation streaks, learning logs, recurring service, contribution frequency, habit tracking.

**Related Community Archetypes:** Infrastructure Steward, Economic Builder, Educator, Health Builder, Organizer.

**Ways to strengthen it:** Choose sustainable commitments, track patterns, build routines with peers, and celebrate small repetitions.

**Suggested learning resources:** Habit formation, personal systems design, health routines, savings discipline, study planning.

**Suggested community activities:** Weekly study groups, recurring mutual aid, savings circles, maintenance teams, practice cohorts.

### Strategic Thinking

**Definition:** The ability to connect present choices to long-term goals, systems, risks, and opportunities.

**Why it matters:** Strategic thinking helps communities move beyond reaction and build durable institutions.

**Observable behaviors:**

- Identifies dependencies and tradeoffs.
- Connects tasks to long-term outcomes.
- Anticipates risks and prepares alternatives.
- Uses evidence and history to guide planning.

**Related Garvey evidence:** Planning responses, project design, systems interests, decision patterns, long-term goal setting.

**Related Community Archetypes:** Economic Builder, Governance Steward, Organizer, Infrastructure Steward, Innovator.

**Ways to strengthen it:** Practice scenario planning, create roadmaps, study systems, review historical case studies, and evaluate outcomes.

**Suggested learning resources:** Strategic planning, systems thinking, cooperative business planning, policy analysis, history of institution building.

**Suggested community activities:** Community planning retreats, budget strategy sessions, cooperative launch planning, risk mapping workshops.

### Conflict Resolution

**Definition:** The ability to address disagreement, harm, and tension in ways that pursue truth, repair, boundaries, and forward movement.

**Why it matters:** Conflict is inevitable. Healthy resolution prevents fragmentation and strengthens trust.

**Observable behaviors:**

- Listens to multiple sides before judging.
- Separates people from problems when appropriate.
- Encourages clear agreements and repair steps.
- Knows when mediation, boundaries, or escalation are needed.

**Related Garvey evidence:** Peer mediation roles, dispute response, feedback history, restorative process participation, communication patterns.

**Related Community Archetypes:** Mediator, Governance Steward, Organizer, Care Builder, Educator.

**Ways to strengthen it:** Learn mediation basics, practice reflective listening, study restorative justice, and use structured debriefs.

**Suggested learning resources:** Mediation training, nonviolent communication, restorative justice, trauma-informed facilitation.

**Suggested community activities:** Peace circles, mediation teams, group agreements, post-event debriefs, restorative conferences.

### Financial Stewardship

**Definition:** Managing money, value, assets, credit, and economic commitments responsibly for personal and collective stability.

**Why it matters:** Economic self-determination requires members who can build, protect, circulate, and govern financial resources with integrity.

**Observable behaviors:**

- Budgets realistically.
- Pays agreed dues or communicates constraints early.
- Tracks shared funds transparently.
- Makes decisions that protect long-term economic health.

**Related Garvey evidence:** Financial learning interests, savings behavior, economic project participation, contribution history, budgeting activity.

**Related Community Archetypes:** Economic Builder, Governance Steward, Infrastructure Steward, Organizer, Land Steward.

**Ways to strengthen it:** Learn budgeting, cooperative finance, bookkeeping, credit repair, investment basics, and transparent reporting.

**Suggested learning resources:** Personal finance courses, cooperative finance guides, bookkeeping basics, credit education, community investment models.

**Suggested community activities:** Savings circles, cooperative budgeting, financial literacy workshops, community investment clubs, business incubators.

### Cultural Preservation

**Definition:** Protecting, practicing, teaching, and transmitting heritage, history, language, memory, art, and identity.

**Why it matters:** Culture provides continuity, dignity, identity, and guidance for future generations.

**Observable behaviors:**

- Participates in cultural learning or teaching.
- Documents stories, rituals, languages, and histories.
- Supports culturally grounded events and education.
- Respects elders and ancestral knowledge.

**Related Garvey evidence:** Cultural interests, history engagement, language learning, archive contributions, event participation.

**Related Community Archetypes:** Cultural Keeper, Educator, Elder Council, Youth Builder, Storyteller.

**Ways to strengthen it:** Study history, learn languages, interview elders, document traditions, and teach younger members.

**Suggested learning resources:** African and diasporic history, language lessons, oral history methods, archival practice, cultural arts.

**Suggested community activities:** Oral history projects, language circles, cultural festivals, archive days, intergenerational storytelling.

### Lifelong Learning

**Definition:** The ongoing practice of seeking knowledge, skills, reflection, and correction throughout life.

**Why it matters:** Lifelong learning keeps members and institutions adaptable, competent, and future-ready.

**Observable behaviors:**

- Enrolls in learning activities.
- Applies feedback to improve.
- Shares useful knowledge with others.
- Connects learning to community needs.

**Related Garvey evidence:** Course participation, reading preferences, skill development, reflection entries, learning streaks.

**Related Community Archetypes:** Educator, Innovator, Economic Builder, Cultural Keeper, Health Builder.

**Ways to strengthen it:** Build study routines, choose learning goals, teach what is learned, and connect learning to active projects.

**Suggested learning resources:** Books, audiobooks, courses, apprenticeships, language lessons, research libraries.

**Suggested community activities:** Study circles, skill shares, reading groups, apprenticeship programs, learning hub cohorts.

### Mentorship

**Definition:** Supporting another person's growth through guidance, encouragement, example, challenge, and practical wisdom.

**Why it matters:** Mentorship transfers knowledge, builds confidence, and strengthens intergenerational continuity.

**Observable behaviors:**

- Provides guidance without controlling another person's path.
- Shares lessons, tools, and opportunities.
- Encourages accountability and growth.
- Makes space for mentees to lead.

**Related Garvey evidence:** Peer support activity, teaching roles, elder-youth engagement, feedback quality, leadership development behavior.

**Related Community Archetypes:** Educator, Elder Council, Youth Builder, Cultural Keeper, Economic Builder.

**Ways to strengthen it:** Learn coaching skills, practice listening, define mentor agreements, and support mentees in real projects.

**Suggested learning resources:** Coaching basics, apprenticeship models, youth development, intergenerational learning, reflective practice.

**Suggested community activities:** Mentor circles, apprenticeship pairings, youth entrepreneurship labs, elder-youth interviews, skill transfer workshops.

## Deliverable 4: Characteristic Growth Framework

The framework should describe development without implementing scores. Future Simba experiences may use levels, stars, bands, progress language, or visual indicators, but this document does not define an algorithm.

### Development Stages

| Stage | Meaning | Suggested Member-Facing Language |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Emerging | Early evidence or interest is present. | "You are beginning to practice this characteristic." |
| Practicing | Repeated attempts and learning activity are visible. | "You are actively strengthening this characteristic." |
| Demonstrating | Consistent evidence appears across activities. | "You currently demonstrate this characteristic in multiple settings." |
| Strengthening Others | The member helps others grow in this area. | "You are helping others develop this characteristic." |
| Stewarding | The member protects, teaches, and institutionalizes the characteristic. | "You are contributing to community systems that preserve this characteristic." |

### Growth Inputs

Future Simba interpretation may consider:

- Self-reflection and stated goals
- Garvey assessment evidence
- Community participation history
- Learning activity
- Volunteer and project contributions
- Peer or mentor feedback
- Completion of recommended actions
- Changes over time, not one-time snapshots

### Growth Outputs

Future member-facing recommendations should include:

- Current development snapshot
- Growth trend
- Suggested next action
- Learning resources
- Community opportunities
- Mentorship or peer support options
- Reflection prompt

### Guardrails

- Do not present characteristics as immutable traits.
- Do not rank human worth.
- Do not punish members for early-stage development.
- Do not infer sensitive attributes or make claims unsupported by evidence.
- Do not let Garvey directly assign Community Characteristics.
- Do not expose raw internal evidence without user-centered explanation and privacy review.

## Deliverable 6: Future Dashboard Concepts

These are design concepts only. They do not define scoring logic.

### Member Growth Snapshot

A dashboard may show a member's current developmental picture in plain language.

Example:

| Characteristic | Current Development | Growth Trend | Suggested Next Step |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Cooperation | Demonstrating | Rising | Join a team-based community project. |
| Leadership | Practicing | Stable | Facilitate one small meeting with mentor support. |
| Financial Stewardship | Emerging | Rising | Complete a budgeting lesson and join a savings circle. |
| Service | Demonstrating | Rising | Choose a recurring monthly service role. |

### Visual Concepts

Possible visual components:

- Characteristic cards with short explanations
- Growth trend arrows or timeline markers
- "Recommended next practice" modules
- Learning pathway tiles
- Community opportunity matches
- Mentor suggestions
- Reflection prompts
- Evidence transparency summary

### Example Star Visualization

Stars may be used as a friendly visualization if they are explained as developmental indicators, not personal worth.

```text
Current Development

Cooperation            ★★★★☆
Leadership             ★★★☆☆
Financial Stewardship  ★★☆☆☆
Service                ★★★★★
```

Any star display should be paired with language such as: "This reflects current evidence and can change as you learn, practice, and contribute."

### Dashboard Guardrails

- Show growth pathways next to current development.
- Avoid shame-based comparisons.
- Prioritize actionable recommendations over static labels.
- Let members see why a recommendation appeared.
- Give members ways to correct, contextualize, or enrich their development profile.
- Keep community opportunity recommendations opt-in and privacy-aware.

- What evidence members can view.

- Whether peer feedback is anonymous, attributed, or summarized.

- How sensitive data is excluded from inappropriate recommendations.

Before implementation, the team should resolve:

1. Which characteristics should be combined, separated, renamed, or removed?
