# Simba Knowledge Commons Architecture

## Purpose

This document defines the Knowledge Commons: the lifelong learning institution of the Simba Community Operating System.

The Knowledge Commons is not simply a digital library, content catalog, course platform, or media shelf. It is the environment where members continuously develop themselves so they can participate meaningfully in family life, community life, cooperative economics, institution building, and intergenerational preservation.

The Knowledge Commons explains how knowledge moves through the Simba ecosystem:

* Books become study.
* Audiobooks become accessible learning.
* Languages become cultural reconnection and communication.
* Brain Training becomes mental discipline.
* Assessments become developmental evidence.
* Reflections become self-knowledge.
* Discussions become shared understanding.
* Mentorship becomes guided growth.
* Practical service becomes demonstrated wisdom.
* Publishing becomes preservation for future generations.

This is a planning-only architecture document. It does not create runtime behavior, APIs, routes, schemas, database tables, scoring logic, Garvey changes, STAR changes, or production workflows.

---

## Foundational Vision

The Knowledge Commons exists to transform information into wisdom, and wisdom into action.

Knowledge should never remain isolated. Learning should naturally lead to reflection. Reflection should lead to practice. Practice should lead to cooperation. Cooperation should strengthen institutions. Institutions should preserve knowledge for future generations.

The Commons therefore follows a simple principle:

> Knowledge is not accumulated for personal status. Knowledge is cultivated so that individuals become capable of strengthening families, communities, institutions, and future generations.

Within Simba, the purpose of education is not passive consumption. A member is not considered developed because they have merely watched, read, listened, or completed a lesson. Learning becomes meaningful when it changes how the member thinks, serves, cooperates, teaches, builds, and preserves.

## Architectural Position in the Simba Ecosystem

The Knowledge Commons sits between individual development and community action.

```text
Library / Audiobooks / Languages / Brain Training / Health Learning
        ↓
Knowledge Commons
        ↓
Reflection / Discussion / Assessment / Pathway Guidance
        ↓
Community Circles / Mentorship / Teach-Back / Practice
        ↓
Community Operations / Marketplace / Investment / Institution Building
        ↓
Publishing / Archives / Community Memory
```

The Commons does not replace existing systems. It connects them into one continuous learning journey.

It provides the conceptual architecture for how a member moves from exposure to understanding, from understanding to practice, from practice to contribution, and from contribution to legacy.

## Knowledge Commons Definition

The Knowledge Commons is Simba's shared learning environment for:

* Personal study.
* Cultural literacy.
* Historical memory.
* Language development.
* Civic and cooperative education.
* Health and wellness literacy.
* Skill development.
* Reflection and self-assessment.
* Peer discussion.
* Mentorship.
* Teach-back.
* Applied community service.
* Research and documentation.
* Publishing and knowledge preservation.

It is called a Commons because knowledge is treated as a shared inheritance and shared responsibility. Members are invited to receive knowledge, test it through practice, improve it through cooperation, and preserve it for others.

The Commons must respect member agency. Members should be guided, not coerced. Recommendations should invite, not force. Assessment evidence should inform, not define. Mentorship should support, not dominate. Knowledge should be offered in ways that honor different ages, learning styles, abilities, languages, and seasons of life.

## Existing System Integrations

### Library

The Library is the primary discovery and study surface for written knowledge. Within the Knowledge Commons, Library resources become more than isolated books. They can conceptually support learning trails, reading groups, reflection prompts, research projects, and institution-building curricula.

The Library should remain a source of intellectual grounding. Future architecture may connect Library materials to member interests, development pathways, Community Characteristics, Community Circles, and Publishing outputs without changing the Library's core purpose.

### Audiobooks

Audiobooks make the Commons more accessible to members who learn while commuting, working, exercising, resting, or managing limited reading time. They also support oral learning traditions and intergenerational listening.

Within the Commons, audiobooks can support shared listening journeys, family listening sessions, discussion prompts, and reflective learning for members who prefer spoken material.

### Swahili

Swahili learning supports cultural reconnection, Pan-African communication, discipline, memory, and identity development. In the Knowledge Commons, Swahili should be understood as both language education and cultural practice.

Future learning journeys may connect Swahili study to history, travel readiness, cultural events, naming traditions, music, storytelling, and cross-community communication.

### Yoruba

Yoruba learning supports cultural memory, spiritual and historical literacy, language discipline, and connection to West African heritage. Within the Commons, Yoruba can serve as both a language pathway and a cultural preservation pathway.

Future learning experiences may connect Yoruba study to oral tradition, family learning, arts, proverbs, historical research, and cultural preservation projects.

### Brain Training

Brain Training supports attention, recall, reasoning, pattern recognition, and mental stamina. Within the Commons, it is not merely entertainment or gamification. It is preparation for deeper study, problem solving, leadership, business judgment, and community responsibility.

Future Commons concepts may align Brain Training exercises with study readiness, reflection habits, problem-solving cohorts, or leadership preparation while preserving existing Brain Training behavior.

### Assessments

Assessments provide developmental evidence. They help members understand strengths, growth areas, interests, tendencies, and possible contribution patterns.

Within the Commons, assessments should not be treated as final labels. They should help answer:

* What might this member benefit from studying next?
* What form of practice might strengthen this member?
* What kind of Circle, mentor, reading group, or project may support growth?
* What reflection questions could help the member understand themselves more clearly?

Garvey observes assessment evidence. The Knowledge Commons may conceptually use that evidence to inform learning recommendations through Simba, but it must not modify Garvey scoring or assessment logic.

### Member Home

Member Home is the personal base of operations. Within the Commons, it can gradually become the member's learning dashboard, reflection journal entry point, recommendation surface, mentorship entry point, and contribution history view.

Member Home should evolve with the member. Early members may see orientation learning and simple study invitations. Growing members may see teach-back prompts, cohort invitations, and project-based learning. Experienced members may see mentorship opportunities, research needs, and preservation projects.

### Development Pathways

Development Pathways connect a member's current state to growth opportunities. Within the Commons, pathways help organize knowledge into meaningful sequences without turning learning into a rigid ladder.

A pathway may include books, audiobooks, language practice, Brain Training, assessment reflection, Circle discussion, community service, mentorship, and Publishing outputs. The pathway should remain an invitation to growth rather than a mandatory gate.

### Community Characteristics

Community Characteristics describe developmental qualities such as cooperation, responsibility, leadership, stewardship, integrity, communication, service, creativity, initiative, and financial stewardship.

The Knowledge Commons supports these characteristics by aligning learning with practice. For example, cooperation is strengthened through group study and shared projects; leadership through teach-back and mentorship; stewardship through community operations; financial stewardship through business and cooperative development education.

### Community Archetypes

Community Archetypes describe contribution models, not permanent identities. The Commons can help members explore archetype-aligned learning without limiting them.

A Knowledge Keeper may study archives and preservation. A Community Builder may study organizing and facilitation. An Economic Builder may study cooperative development and financial literacy. A Cultural Guardian may study language, history, and arts. A Systems Builder may study technology, operations, and governance.

### Community Circles

Community Circles turn individual learning into collective reflection. They are where reading, listening, language practice, and research become conversation, accountability, mutual support, and shared interpretation.

The Commons should treat Circles as learning environments, not only social groups. A Circle may host a reading group, language partner rotation, teach-back session, workshop, or community research effort.

### Community Operations

Community Operations apply knowledge to real needs. Preparedness, mutual aid, volunteer coordination, events, projects, and service opportunities are where members test learning through responsibility.

The Commons should encourage movement from study into service. A member who studies preparedness should practice it. A member who studies governance should participate responsibly. A member who studies health should strengthen household and community wellness habits. A member who studies cooperative economics should help build practical systems.

### Publishing

Publishing preserves learning. It converts reflection, research, teach-back, field experience, oral history, and institutional knowledge into durable artifacts.

Within the Commons, Publishing is the bridge from individual insight to community memory. Future Publishing flows may preserve study guides, community reports, historical archives, research briefs, mentorship notes, workshop materials, and institutional playbooks.

### STAR

STAR recognizes participation and contribution. Within the Commons, STAR should remain recognition, not the reason for learning.

Future architecture may recognize teach-back, workshop participation, reading groups, mentorship, research projects, and preservation work. However, STAR should never reduce wisdom to points or create unhealthy status competition.

### PocketPT

PocketPT supports health learning where appropriate. Physical well-being affects study capacity, emotional regulation, leadership stamina, family responsibility, and long-term service.

Within the Commons, PocketPT-related learning can support health literacy, movement education, rehabilitation awareness, preventive care habits, and responsible body stewardship. It should remain within appropriate health boundaries and should not be converted into unrelated academic scoring.

## Conceptual Learning Cycle

The Knowledge Commons organizes learning as a lifelong cycle:

```text
Discover
  ↓
Study
  ↓
Reflect
  ↓
Practice
  ↓
Demonstrate
  ↓
Share
  ↓
Mentor
  ↓
Preserve
  ↓
Repeat
```

### Discover

Members encounter knowledge through Library resources, audiobooks, language materials, Member Home recommendations, Circle invitations, community needs, mentor suggestions, assessments, PocketPT health education, or Publishing archives.

### Study

Members engage the material through reading, listening, language practice, drills, courses, workshops, or guided learning trails.

### Reflect

Members interpret what they learned. Reflection may include journals, assessment review, discussion prompts, personal commitments, family conversations, or mentor-guided questions.

### Practice

Members apply learning in daily life. Practice may occur through language conversations, family budgeting, health routines, cooperative planning, preparedness exercises, governance participation, or community service.

### Demonstrate

Members show learning through action. Demonstration may include completing a project, facilitating a discussion, helping a Circle, teaching a concept, contributing to operations, or producing a useful artifact.

### Share

Members make knowledge useful to others through discussion, teach-back, workshops, notes, guides, peer support, or collaborative research.

### Mentor

Members who have practiced responsibly help others grow. Mentorship should be relational, humble, accountable, and rooted in service rather than status.

### Preserve

Members and institutions preserve knowledge through Publishing, archives, playbooks, oral histories, research collections, study guides, and community memory projects.

### Repeat

Learning never ends. Members return to earlier stages as new responsibilities, seasons of life, community needs, and institutional opportunities emerge.

## Community Learning Patterns

The Commons expands beyond individual study through shared learning forms.

### Discussion Circles

Discussion Circles help members interpret material together, compare lived experience, ask better questions, and develop collective understanding.

### Reading Groups

Reading Groups create disciplined study rhythms around books, articles, documents, and historical texts. They should encourage comprehension, reflection, and practical application.

### Language Partners

Language Partners support consistent practice in Swahili, Yoruba, or future language pathways. Partners should encourage accountability, pronunciation practice, cultural context, and confidence.

### Study Cohorts

Study Cohorts help members move through a topic together over time. Cohorts may focus on leadership, financial literacy, health, preparedness, governance, business, agriculture, technology, or institution building.

### Community Workshops

Workshops translate learning into usable skill. They may include facilitation practice, health education, emergency readiness, cooperative business planning, family financial systems, publishing skills, or archival methods.

### Mentorship

Mentorship connects experienced members with growing members. Mentors help interpret knowledge, model practice, encourage discipline, and connect learning to service.

### Teach-Back Sessions

Teach-back turns learners into contributors. A member who studies a topic may be invited to explain it to others, lead a short session, create a guide, or support a cohort.

### Collaborative Research

Collaborative research allows members to investigate community needs, historical questions, business opportunities, health patterns, governance models, or institution-building strategies.

### Community Projects

Projects convert learning into tangible service. A project might create a preparedness checklist, community garden plan, oral history collection, youth reading circle, cooperative business proposal, or family finance workshop.

### Knowledge Preservation

Knowledge Preservation protects what the community learns. It includes archives, Publishing outputs, recorded oral histories, study guides, project reports, institutional playbooks, and community memory.

## Relationship to Other Systems

The Knowledge Commons clarifies how learning flows across Simba:

* Garvey observes learning-related evidence through assessments and developmental signals.
* Simba recommends learning based on member goals, pathways, characteristics, archetypes, and community opportunities.
* Publishing preserves learning so insights do not disappear with individual memory.
* Community Circles discuss learning and turn private study into shared understanding.
* Community Operations apply learning through projects, preparedness, mutual aid, service, and institutional practice.
* Marketplace creates opportunity from learning by connecting skills, services, businesses, and cooperative value creation.
* Investment expands long-term impact by helping mature learning become durable assets and institutions.
* PocketPT develops physical well-being so members can sustain study, service, family responsibility, and leadership over time.

These relationships should remain conceptually distinct. Observation, recommendation, discussion, application, recognition, commerce, investment, health, and preservation should reinforce one another without collapsing into one monolithic system.

## Member Experience Over Time

### Early Members: Learning and Orientation

Early members primarily need orientation, trust, rhythm, and accessible entry points. Member Home may emphasize introductory Library materials, audiobooks, language practice, Brain Training, health literacy, basic assessments, and simple reflection prompts.

The goal is not to overwhelm the member. The goal is to help the member begin a sustainable learning habit.

### Growing Members: Reflection and Practice

Growing members begin connecting study to action. They may join a reading group, language partner session, Circle discussion, workshop, or community service project.

Member Home may begin showing pathway-aligned invitations, reflection reminders, Circle opportunities, and practical next steps.

### Contributing Members: Teaching and Cooperation

Contributing members begin sharing what they have learned. They may lead a short teach-back, support a cohort, help with a workshop, contribute to Publishing, or apply learning in Community Operations.

The Commons should help these members develop humility, clarity, reliability, and cooperative habits.

### Experienced Members: Mentorship

Experienced members help others navigate learning, practice, and contribution. They may mentor new members, guide cohorts, support Community Circles, review research, or help translate study into operations.

Mentorship should be grounded in service and accountability, not hierarchy.

### Community Leaders: Preservation and Institution Stewardship

Community leaders help preserve knowledge and strengthen institutions. They may organize archives, produce playbooks, steward curricula, support Publishing standards, facilitate research initiatives, and connect learning to long-term community strategy.

### Legacy Members: Institution Building

Legacy members help turn accumulated knowledge into durable institutions: schools, funds, farms, health initiatives, businesses, archives, media systems, youth programs, elder councils, cooperative structures, and leadership pipelines.

Their role is not merely to be remembered. Their role is to ensure the next generation inherits stronger systems.

## Architectural Decisions

1. **The Knowledge Commons is an institution, not a feature.** It provides a conceptual home for learning across the ecosystem rather than replacing Library, Audiobooks, Languages, Brain Training, Assessments, Member Home, Circles, Operations, Publishing, STAR, or PocketPT.

2. **Learning is cyclical, not linear.** The Discover → Study → Reflect → Practice → Demonstrate → Share → Mentor → Preserve → Repeat cycle should guide future experiences without becoming a rigid funnel.

3. **Knowledge must lead toward service.** The architecture prioritizes practical wisdom, cooperation, and institution building over passive content consumption.

4. **Garvey remains observational.** Garvey may observe assessment evidence, but this document does not alter Garvey scoring, interpretation, or APIs.

5. **Simba remains developmental.** Simba may recommend learning conceptually, but recommendations should respect agency, consent, transparency, and member context.

6. **Publishing is the preservation layer.** Knowledge that has been tested, taught, researched, or practiced should be preserved through Publishing and archives where appropriate.

7. **STAR remains recognition, not motivation.** STAR may eventually recognize learning contributions, but wisdom must not be reduced to points or status competition.

8. **Health learning belongs within whole-person development.** PocketPT-related learning should support physical well-being and responsible participation without overstepping health boundaries.

9. **Community learning is essential.** Discussion, cohorts, mentorship, projects, and preservation are first-class parts of the architecture, not optional add-ons.

10. **No production behavior changes are introduced by this document.** The architecture is intentionally planning-only.

## Future Implementation Recommendations

When the ecosystem is ready for implementation planning, future teams should consider:

1. Designing member-controlled learning profiles that respect privacy and consent.
2. Connecting Library and Audiobook materials to optional reflection prompts.
3. Creating lightweight learning trail metadata before building complex recommendation systems.
4. Piloting reading groups and language partner flows manually before automating them.
5. Defining clear boundaries between Garvey observation and Simba recommendation.
6. Treating assessment-informed learning as explainable guidance, never as a fixed identity.
7. Creating Publishing standards for study guides, research briefs, oral histories, and institutional playbooks.
8. Developing mentor preparation guidelines before launching formal mentorship matching.
9. Ensuring STAR recognition supports contribution without creating unhealthy competition.
10. Integrating PocketPT health learning only where it supports whole-person development and remains appropriate to health guidance boundaries.
11. Supporting accessibility through audio, plain language summaries, multilingual pathways, and low-bandwidth formats.
12. Creating governance for archival quality, cultural sensitivity, historical accuracy, and community consent.
