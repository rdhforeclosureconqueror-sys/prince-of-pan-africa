# Community Circle Engine

## Purpose

The Community Circle Engine is the planned organizational backbone for Simba. Garvey helps the ecosystem understand individuals, Simba helps individuals grow, and the Community Circle Engine will help individuals cooperate.

A Circle is a small, trust-centered team of members who coordinate around shared community goals. The engine exists to help future Simba systems form healthy, balanced teams where different strengths complement one another.

The Community Circle Engine is **not** a personality grouping system. It is a community balancing system. Its final purpose is to help people discover how their unique strengths can work together so the Circle can accomplish more than any member could alone.

This document is planning and architecture only. It does not implement matching logic, member assignment, member ranking, Circle scoring, or production behavior.

## Core philosophy

A healthy community should be treated like a healthy ecosystem. Healthy ecosystems do not depend on sameness; they depend on complementary diversity, mutual responsibility, and balance.

Future Circles should aim for a healthy mix of strengths such as:

- Vision
- Organization
- Empathy
- Leadership
- Consistency
- Creativity
- Communication
- Service
- Strategic thinking
- Stewardship
- Accountability
- Initiative

The engine should eventually help identify whether a Circle has enough complementary coverage to work well together. It should never reduce a member to a fixed identity, permanent label, or static role. Community Archetypes and Community Characteristics are evidence that can help balance a Circle, not definitions that limit a person.

## Garvey / Simba boundary

Garvey remains the observation engine. Simba remains the coordination and growth environment.

Garvey may continue to:

- Collect assessments
- Identify archetypes
- Identify characteristics
- Identify strengths
- Identify growth areas
- Produce observations and evidence

Garvey must not:

- Create Circles
- Recommend social placement
- Assign community roles
- Automatically match members
- Rank members for Circle placement
- Convert assessment results into permanent social labels

Simba may eventually receive Garvey observations and interpret them in the context of community cooperation. That interpretation should be explicit, reviewable, and framed around opportunities for balance rather than judgments about people.

## Circle model

A Circle is a small cooperative unit where members know one another, practice trust, coordinate action, learn together, and contribute to community outcomes.

A future Circle may support concepts such as:

- Circle name
- Circle purpose
- Circle mission
- Circle members
- Circle facilitator
- Community Operations participation
- Preparedness participation
- Community projects
- Shared learning
- Shared goals
- Community achievements
- STAR participation
- Community milestones

Circles should remain intentionally small. The exact future size should be determined through community testing, but the design assumption is that Circles work best when every member can be known, heard, and accountable to the group.

### Conceptual Circle lifecycle

A future implementation may define stages such as:

1. **Proposed** — a Circle idea or need has been identified.
2. **Forming** — members are invited or self-selecting, with human review.
3. **Active** — the Circle has a mission, facilitator, and shared activities.
4. **Growing** — members are learning, rotating responsibilities, and developing new strengths.
5. **Evolving** — the Circle changes mission, adds members, graduates members into new responsibilities, or splits into new Circles when healthy.
6. **Archived** — the Circle has completed its purpose or is no longer active, while preserving history respectfully.

These stages are planning references only and should not be implemented until product requirements are approved.

## Circle balance philosophy

The engine should evaluate Circle balance, not score people.

A Circle may eventually be reviewed for coverage across cooperative capacities such as:

- Leadership diversity
- Communication coverage
- Stewardship coverage
- Reliability coverage
- Initiative coverage
- Learning participation
- Volunteer participation
- Community engagement
- Preparedness participation
- Mentorship availability
- Conflict-resolution capacity
- Operational follow-through

Circle balance should be interpreted as a group-level planning signal. It should not become a hidden individual score or ranking mechanism.

### Opportunity-oriented language

Future Circle recommendations should always be framed as opportunities rather than deficiencies. Examples:

- “This Circle has many vision-oriented members and may benefit from stronger logistical organization.”
- “This Circle would benefit from members who are actively practicing consistency.”
- “This Circle has strong leadership energy and may be ready to develop more mentorship capacity.”
- “This Circle shows strong preparedness activity and may have an opportunity to deepen broader community participation.”

Language should avoid shame, hierarchy, or fixed identity. The goal is to guide healthy cooperation.

## Future Community Balance Engine

The future Community Balance Engine should be a Simba-side planning layer that helps community leaders, facilitators, and members see opportunities for better cooperation.

Its purpose should be to:

- Interpret Circle-level balance signals
- Identify missing or underrepresented cooperative capacities
- Highlight complementary strengths already present in the Circle
- Suggest development opportunities
- Suggest learning, service, preparedness, or project pathways
- Support human review and member agency

Its purpose should not be to:

- Judge members
- Rank members
- Automatically assign members
- Automatically remove members
- Create hidden social scores
- Convert Garvey observations into destiny
- Replace community leadership or member consent

### Conceptual balance inputs

Future balance analysis may consider evidence from:

- Garvey observations
- Community Characteristics
- Community Archetypes
- Member-stated interests
- Member consent and availability
- Community Operations participation
- Preparedness activity
- Learning progress
- Volunteer history
- Project participation
- Facilitator observations
- Member self-reflection

Any implementation should distinguish between observed evidence, self-declared preferences, facilitator notes, and system-derived interpretations.

## Characteristic integration

Community Characteristics may eventually contribute to Circle balance as flexible, developmental signals.

Examples include:

- Cooperation
- Integrity
- Responsibility
- Reliability
- Service
- Compassion
- Communication
- Stewardship
- Discipline
- Initiative
- Conflict Resolution
- Adaptability
- Leadership
- Financial Stewardship
- Mentorship

These characteristics should influence Circle balance without becoming member labels. A member who demonstrates Reliability in one context may still be developing Communication in another. A member who is currently developing Leadership may later become a mentor. The system should preserve this movement.

### Characteristic design principles

Future implementations should:

- Treat characteristics as evidence of practice, not identity.
- Store context when a characteristic is observed.
- Allow characteristics to grow, decay, change, or be superseded by new evidence.
- Let members see and understand meaningful observations about themselves.
- Avoid presenting characteristics as immutable personality traits.
- Avoid using characteristics as eligibility gates unless a human-governed policy explicitly requires it.

## Community Archetype integration

Community Archetypes may eventually help Circles understand complementary strengths. They should never be used to imply that one archetype is superior to another.

Archetypes should be interpreted as lenses for contribution, not social ranks. For example, a Circle may be strengthened when members who naturally generate vision cooperate with members who organize details, members who nurture relationships, members who steward resources, and members who keep the work consistent.

Future guidance should explain how archetypes can strengthen one another when working toward shared community goals:

- Vision-oriented members may help a Circle imagine what is possible.
- Organization-oriented members may help convert ideas into plans.
- Service-oriented members may keep the Circle connected to community needs.
- Stewardship-oriented members may protect resources and continuity.
- Communication-oriented members may help the Circle stay aligned.
- Mentorship-oriented members may help new members grow into responsibility.
- Initiative-oriented members may help the Circle begin work instead of waiting indefinitely.

These examples are conceptual. They should not become permanent member categories.

## Growth philosophy

Members should not remain fixed forever. The Community Circle Engine should be built around continuous development.

Future Circle systems should assume that people:

- Learn through practice
- Grow through responsibility
- Develop through mentorship
- Mature through service
- Build confidence through participation
- Expand their strengths over time
- Can embody new characteristics in new seasons

The objective is continuous development rather than permanent classification. Circles should become places where members practice new capacities, not places where the system locks members into old assessment outcomes.

### Role rotation and mentorship

Future Circle design should support human-led role rotation and mentorship. A member who is not currently comfortable facilitating may eventually co-facilitate. A member learning communication may practice reporting project updates. A member developing stewardship may help track shared resources under guidance.

The engine should encourage safe growth pathways rather than fixed assignments.

## Privacy considerations

Circle systems will involve social context and therefore require careful privacy design.

Future implementations should:

- Use the least visibility required for coordination.
- Avoid exposing raw assessment data to Circle members by default.
- Avoid showing sensitive growth areas in group dashboards.
- Allow members to understand how observations are used.
- Record consent before using member data for Circle recommendations.
- Distinguish facilitator-visible information from member-visible information.
- Treat household, health, emergency, family, financial, and location-sensitive data as sensitive.
- Provide audit trails for administrative Circle changes.
- Avoid hidden social scoring.
- Avoid permanent labels in public or member-facing interfaces.

## Architectural decisions

This planning document establishes the following decisions:

1. The Community Circle Engine belongs to Simba, not Garvey.
2. Garvey observations may inform future Circle balance, but Garvey must not create or assign Circles.
3. Circle balance is group-level and opportunity-oriented.
4. Members are developing individuals, not fixed personality types.
5. Community Characteristics are contextual evidence, not permanent labels.
6. Community Archetypes are complementary strengths, not ranks.
7. Future recommendations must preserve member agency and require human review.
8. Future dashboards should focus on mission, activity, growth, and impact rather than scores.
9. Future APIs and data models are conceptual until privacy, consent, and governance are approved.
10. No production behavior changes are authorized by this document.

## Future implementation recommendations

Before implementation, the team should define:

- Member consent requirements for Circle recommendations.
- Facilitator permissions and responsibilities.
- Circle size guidelines.
- Circle creation and invitation workflows.
- Member self-selection and opt-out workflows.
- Privacy levels for Circle information.
- Human review requirements for recommendations.
- Dashboard visibility rules.
- Audit logging requirements.
- Compatibility with Community Operations and Preparedness.
- Whether STAR participation is displayed only or can eventually connect to Circle milestones.
- A non-production prototype plan that does not assign real members automatically.

The first implementation should likely be a read-only or facilitator-managed Circle planning surface, not an automatic placement engine.

## Final principle

The purpose of the Community Circle Engine is not to organize people by personality.

Its purpose is to help people discover how their unique strengths can complement the strengths of others, so that together they can accomplish more than any individual could alone.
