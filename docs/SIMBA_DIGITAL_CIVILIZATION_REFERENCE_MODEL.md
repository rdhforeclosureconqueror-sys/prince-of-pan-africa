# Simba Digital Civilization Reference Model

## Planning-Only Status

This document is the highest-level ecosystem reference model for Simba as a digital civilization. It explains the purpose, layers, institutions, information flow, member transformation, future expansion logic, and long-term architectural tests for the ecosystem.

This document does **not** implement code, runtime behavior, APIs, routes, database changes, authentication changes, permission changes, Garvey logic, Simba logic, STAR behavior, Marketplace behavior, Publishing behavior, PocketPT behavior, Investment Platform behavior, or user-facing functionality.

---

## 1. Philosophy

Simba is not a collection of software applications. Simba is an ecosystem of cooperating institutions.

Software exists only to support those institutions. A dashboard is not the mission. An assessment is not the mission. A marketplace is not the mission. A reward system is not the mission. Each tool matters only insofar as it strengthens learning, dignity, cooperation, health, preparedness, business, ownership, culture, and legacy.

The digital civilization model reframes the architecture from feature delivery to institution building. A feature asks, "What can the system do?" An institution asks, "What responsibility does this part of the ecosystem carry over time?"

The purpose of this reference model is to make the whole ecosystem understandable to new developers, community leaders, board members, contributors, and future generations.

## 2. Civilization Model

```text
Mission
  ↓
Constitution
  ↓
Architecture
  ↓
Institutions
  ↓
Systems
  ↓
Members
  ↓
Communities
  ↓
Businesses
  ↓
Ownership
  ↓
Legacy
```

### 2.1 Mission

The mission defines why Simba exists: to strengthen people and communities through learning, cooperation, preparedness, culture, wellness, business, ownership, and legacy. The mission is the reason every institution exists.

### 2.2 Constitution

The Constitution expresses the ecosystem's core commitments. It protects dignity, agency, community benefit, consent, stewardship, transparency, and mission-first development. It is the ethical foundation beneath architecture.

### 2.3 Architecture

Architecture translates the mission and Constitution into durable structures. It defines boundaries, responsibilities, dependencies, decision frameworks, lifecycle patterns, and institutional relationships.

### 2.4 Institutions

Institutions carry long-term responsibilities. Knowledge Commons teaches. Garvey observes. Simba interprets. Community Circles cooperate. Marketplace circulates opportunity. Publishing preserves. Investment builds ownership. STAR recognizes. Preparedness strengthens resilience.

### 2.5 Systems

Systems are the software, workflows, data structures, interfaces, reports, and operational tools that support institutions. Systems should never become more important than the institutions they serve.

### 2.6 Members

Members are whole people with agency, privacy, consent, gifts, needs, responsibilities, and capacity for growth. Simba should support members without reducing them to scores, labels, roles, transactions, or engagement metrics.

### 2.7 Communities

Communities are the relational spaces where members learn, practice, cooperate, serve, organize, prepare, and build trust. Communities transform individual development into shared capacity.

### 2.8 Businesses

Businesses convert skill, trust, and cooperation into goods, services, employment, enterprise, and economic circulation. Business systems should strengthen community resilience and ethical opportunity.

### 2.9 Ownership

Ownership is the long-term economic and institutional stake that allows communities to preserve value, direct resources, and build wealth responsibly. Ownership should be patient, ethical, transparent, and mission-aligned.

### 2.10 Legacy

Legacy is the transfer of wisdom, institutions, culture, ownership, practices, stories, and responsibility to future generations. Legacy is the test of whether the digital civilization outlives its first builders.

## 4. Information Flow

Information should move through Simba in ways that preserve ownership, dignity, context, and consent.

```text
Garvey observes.
  ↓
Simba interprets.
  ↓
Knowledge Commons teaches.
  ↓
Development Pathways recommend.
  ↓
Community Circles cooperate.
  ↓
Community Operations activate.
  ↓
Marketplace creates opportunity.
  ↓
Investment creates ownership.
  ↓
Publishing preserves.
  ↓
PocketPT strengthens.
  ↓
Legacy carries everything forward.
```

### 4.1 Garvey Observes

Garvey gathers and organizes assessment evidence. It should make observations clear, limited, and contextual. It should not control member identity or future opportunity.

### 4.2 Simba Interprets

Simba converts evidence into invitations, recommendations, and pathways. Interpretation must preserve member agency and explain why a recommendation exists.

### 4.3 Knowledge Commons Teaches

Knowledge Commons provides the learning resources that help members understand, practice, reflect, and teach others. It turns recommendations into educational pathways.

### 4.4 Development Pathways Recommend

Development Pathways connect member goals, observed patterns, learning resources, Circle opportunities, service needs, and mentorship options into practical next steps.

### 4.5 Community Circles Cooperate

Community Circles provide relational spaces where learning becomes trust, mutual aid, practice, and contribution.

### 4.6 Community Operations Activate

Community Operations turns Circle capacity and institutional priorities into real projects, service, preparedness work, events, and community execution.

### 4.7 Marketplace Creates Opportunity

Marketplace helps members and businesses exchange value, meet needs, discover services, and circulate resources ethically within the community.

### 4.8 Investment Creates Ownership

Investment transforms business opportunity and community trust into long-term ownership, wealth building, and responsible stewardship.

### 4.9 Publishing Preserves

Publishing captures stories, lessons, research, education, and institutional memory so knowledge survives beyond temporary conversations and software interfaces.

### 4.10 PocketPT Strengthens

PocketPT strengthens physical readiness, health habits, and resilience so members can participate in community life over the long term.

### 4.11 Legacy Carries Everything Forward

Legacy receives the preserved knowledge, institutions, practices, ownership, stories, and responsibilities of the ecosystem and makes them available to future generations.

## 5. Transformation Journey

Simba's lifelong journey is developmental, not transactional. Members may move through stages in different orders and may return to earlier practices throughout life.

```text
Visitor
  ↓
Member
  ↓
Learner
  ↓
Practitioner
  ↓
Contributor
  ↓
Cooperator
  ↓
Leader
  ↓
Mentor
  ↓
Institution Builder
  ↓
Legacy Keeper
```

### 5.1 Visitor

A visitor encounters the mission, public learning, cultural identity, and invitation to join. The visitor should understand that Simba is a community ecosystem, not only a website.

### 5.2 Member

A member joins intentionally and receives orientation into dignity, agency, privacy, consent, learning, responsibility, and available institutions.

### 5.3 Learner

A learner studies through the Knowledge Commons, reflects on goals, explores history and culture, and begins to see personal growth as connected to community benefit.

### 5.4 Practitioner

A practitioner turns learning into habit, skill, wellness, preparedness, communication, service, and real-world practice.

### 5.5 Contributor

A contributor gives time, knowledge, service, business value, stories, mentorship, operational help, or resources to strengthen the community.

### 5.6 Cooperator

A cooperator works with others through Circles, projects, mutual aid, Marketplace participation, preparedness activity, and shared responsibility.

### 5.7 Leader

A leader accepts responsibility for coordination, care, clarity, trust, and execution. Leadership is measured by service and stewardship rather than status.

### 5.8 Mentor

A mentor helps others learn, practice, interpret experience, avoid mistakes, and grow into responsibility. Mentorship converts personal growth into community continuity.

### 5.9 Institution Builder

An institution builder strengthens or creates durable structures that can serve beyond one person, one project, or one software release.

### 5.10 Legacy Keeper

A legacy keeper preserves knowledge, culture, ownership, practices, archives, stories, institutions, and values for future generations.

## 6. Twenty-Year Test

Every major architectural decision should be tested against a twenty-year horizon:

* Would this architecture still make sense twenty years from now?
* Would new institutions fit naturally into the model?
* Would future developers understand why the system exists?
* Could the community preserve the institution without its founders?
* Would the architecture remain mission-first instead of platform-first?
* Would member dignity, consent, and agency still be protected?
* Would knowledge and institutional memory survive staff turnover and technology changes?
* Would this decision make the ecosystem easier or harder to inherit?

A decision that fails the twenty-year test may still be useful as a temporary implementation detail, but it should not become a foundational architectural pattern.

---

## 7. Future Expansion

Future institutions should emerge naturally from the mission, Constitution, and institutional ecosystem. They should not become disconnected features simply because software can be built.

Every future proposal should answer:

* Which institution owns this?
* Does an existing institution already solve this?
* Does this strengthen the community?
* Does this preserve dignity?
* Does this protect privacy and consent?
* Does this create long-term value?
* Does this strengthen cooperation between institutions?
* Does this add complexity that is worth maintaining?
* What would happen if this proposal disappeared in five years?
* How would future developers and community leaders understand it?

A proposal should become a feature only after it is clear which institution it strengthens. A proposal should become a new institution only when it carries a durable responsibility that no existing institution can responsibly own.

## 8. Executive Summary

### 8.1 Overall Maturity of the Simba Architecture

The Simba architecture is mature at the conceptual and institutional level. The existing documents define constitutional principles, architecture decisions, member lifecycle, Knowledge Commons, Community Operating System concepts, ecosystem mapping, capability inventory, dependency relationships, pilot readiness, and user journeys. This reference model adds the top-level civilizational frame that connects those documents into one coherent whole.

### 8.2 Readiness to Transition from Architecture to Implementation

Simba is ready to transition from broad architecture into carefully sequenced implementation, provided implementation remains institution-first. The next phase should not be unrestricted feature building. It should be disciplined delivery of the smallest member-facing workflows that validate the institutional model.

### 8.3 Strengths of the Institutional Model

The strongest architectural qualities are:

* Clear separation between mission, Constitution, architecture, institutions, systems, and member experience.
* Strong protection against reducing members to scores, labels, or transactions.
* Durable institutional roles for learning, assessment, development, cooperation, economic activity, wellness, publishing, ownership, recognition, and preparedness.
* Natural support for long-term knowledge preservation and intergenerational continuity.
* A model that allows future institutions to be added without losing the core mission.

### 8.4 Potential Architectural Risks

Potential risks include:

* Institutional overlap if ownership boundaries are not maintained.
* Excessive complexity if every idea becomes a new feature or institution.
* Centralization risk if Simba or Member Home becomes the controller of all other institutions.
* Measurement risk if Garvey or STAR outputs are treated as identity labels or status rankings.
* Documentation drift if implementation decisions are not reflected back into architectural references.
* Privacy risk if information flow expands faster than consent and stewardship practices.

### 8.5 Recommended Next Implementation Phase

The recommended next implementation phase is a minimal institutional operating loop:

1. Member orientation and Member Home context.
2. Knowledge Commons learning path access.
3. Consent-aware Garvey assessment flow.
4. Simba development pathway recommendations.
5. Community Circle invitation or participation path.
6. STAR recognition for constructive participation.
7. Publishing of lessons learned from the pilot.

This phase validates the core loop from learning to reflection, assessment, recommendation, cooperation, recognition, and preservation without prematurely implementing the full economic or investment stack.

### 8.6 Recommended Documentation Improvements

Recommended documentation improvements include:

* A single documentation index that identifies which document to read first.
* A glossary of institutional terms, member stages, characteristics, archetypes, and stewardship language.
* A lightweight dependency map that is updated after each implementation phase.
* A consent and privacy architecture reference tied to information flow.
* A pilot learning log that records what changed after real member use.

### 8.7 Opportunities to Simplify

Simplification opportunities include:

* Keep Member Home as a coordination surface, not an owner of every workflow.
* Treat Characteristics and Archetypes as shared developmental language rather than separate product silos.
* Avoid duplicating recommendation logic across Knowledge Commons, Simba, STAR, and Community Circles.
* Use one institutional review rhythm rather than separate review processes for every subsystem.
* Delay advanced Marketplace, Investment, and Business System complexity until the core learning-to-contribution loop is validated.

### 8.8 Opportunities to Strengthen Institutional Cooperation

Cooperation can be strengthened by defining explicit handoffs:

* Garvey to Simba: evidence becomes interpretation.
* Simba to Knowledge Commons: recommendations become learning.
* Knowledge Commons to Circles: learning becomes practice.
* Circles to Community Operations: trust becomes action.
* Operations to Publishing: lessons become preserved knowledge.
* Marketplace to Investment: opportunity becomes ownership.
* STAR to Mentorship: recognition becomes responsibility.

### 8.9 Long-Term Sustainability Assessment

The long-term sustainability outlook is strong if Simba preserves institutional boundaries, keeps documentation current, protects privacy and consent, and resists feature drift. The architecture is designed for continuity because it emphasizes mission, stewardship, knowledge preservation, institutional ownership, and intergenerational legacy.

### 8.10 Overall Assessment

Simba is best understood as both a Community Operating System and a Digital Civilization framework. As a Community Operating System, it coordinates learning, development, cooperation, operations, wellness, economic exchange, and recognition. As a Digital Civilization framework, it organizes institutions around mission, Constitution, ownership, memory, and legacy.

The architecture is ready for implementation only if implementation remains faithful to the central thesis: software must serve institutions, institutions must serve members and communities, and the entire ecosystem must remain accountable to dignity, cooperation, ownership, and legacy.
