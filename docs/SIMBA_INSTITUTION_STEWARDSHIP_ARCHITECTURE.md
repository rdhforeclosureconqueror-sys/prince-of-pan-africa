# Simba Institution Stewardship Architecture

## Planning-Only Status

This document defines the architectural stewardship layer for the Simba ecosystem. It is a planning reference for long-term institutional health, constitutional alignment, and continuous improvement.

This document does **not** implement code, runtime behavior, APIs, routes, database changes, authentication changes, permission changes, Garvey logic, Simba logic, STAR behavior, Marketplace behavior, Publishing behavior, PocketPT behavior, Investment Platform behavior, or user-facing functionality.

---

## 1. Purpose

The Simba ecosystem is composed of institutions, not isolated features. Each institution has a mission, a domain of responsibility, a relationship to members, dependencies on other institutions, and a long-term role in the community.

The purpose of the Institution Stewardship Architecture is to help every institution continually improve while remaining aligned with the Simba Community Constitution. Stewardship evaluates the health of institutions rather than the worth, identity, or status of individual members.

Stewardship is therefore distinct from governance, moderation, administration, enforcement, or operational management. Governance may define authority. Moderation may protect spaces. Administration may execute workflows. Stewardship asks a different question:

> Is this institution becoming healthier, more useful, more dignified, more cooperative, and more sustainable over time?

The stewardship layer exists to make institutional health visible so future builders can preserve what works, repair what weakens, and evolve the ecosystem without drifting away from the mission.

---

## 2. Stewardship Principles

### 2.1 Mission Alignment

Every institution should be able to explain how its work strengthens members, families, communities, businesses, ownership, learning, culture, preparedness, or legacy. If an institution cannot connect its activity to the mission, its architecture should be reviewed.

### 2.2 Member Dignity

Institutions should treat members as whole people with agency, privacy, consent, and capacity for growth. No institution should reduce a member to a score, label, purchase history, assessment result, archetype, role, or transaction.

### 2.3 Community Benefit

Institutional success should be measured by community benefit, not only activity volume. Growth is healthy when it increases trust, learning, cooperation, resilience, opportunity, wellness, ownership, and intergenerational value.

### 2.4 Institutional Cooperation

Institutions should strengthen one another without collapsing into one another. Knowledge Commons teaches, Garvey observes, Simba interprets, Community Circles cooperate, Marketplace circulates opportunity, Publishing preserves, Investment builds ownership, and STAR recognizes positive participation.

### 2.5 Long-Term Sustainability

Institutions should be designed for continuity. A healthy institution can survive staff transitions, changing technology, new contributors, and future expansion because its purpose, boundaries, dependencies, and review practices are documented.

### 2.6 Knowledge Preservation

The ecosystem should preserve lessons, decisions, failures, stories, practices, and institutional memory. Knowledge that is not preserved becomes dependent on individuals; knowledge that is preserved becomes community infrastructure.

### 2.7 Transparency

Members and stewards should be able to understand what an institution does, what it does not do, what information it uses, what outputs it creates, and what decisions remain in human hands.

### 2.8 Privacy

Institutional health should never require unnecessary exposure of member data. Stewardship should prefer aggregate patterns, documented outcomes, consent-aware reporting, and institutional indicators over individual surveillance.

### 2.9 Consent

Where institutional improvement depends on member participation, feedback, assessment, storytelling, health information, economic participation, or publishing, consent must remain visible and revocable according to the relevant institution's design.

### 2.10 Stewardship over Control

Stewardship improves institutions by observing, documenting, recommending, and supporting repair. It does not centrally command all institutions or override their proper responsibilities.

### 2.11 Institution Building

The ecosystem should reward the creation of durable institutions rather than endless feature accumulation. A feature is healthy when it clearly belongs to an institution and strengthens that institution's mission.

### 2.12 Intergenerational Thinking

Architectural decisions should be evaluated by whether future members, leaders, developers, and families can inherit them. Simba should be understandable and maintainable beyond its founding generation.

### 2.13 Continuous Improvement

No institution should be considered finished. Each institution should periodically review what is working, what is confusing, what is harmful, what is missing, what should be simplified, and what should be preserved.

## 3. Institutional Health Indicators

The following indicators describe what healthy institutions look like. They are not automated scores or enforcement rules. They are stewardship prompts for review, reflection, planning, and improvement.

### 3.1 Knowledge Commons

A healthy Knowledge Commons shows active learning, high-quality resources, continuous publishing, teach-back loops, cultural preservation, accessible pathways, and visible knowledge preservation. It helps members turn information into wisdom and wisdom into practice.

Healthy indicators include:

* Members regularly engage with learning resources.
* Resources remain accurate, useful, and mission-aligned.
* New knowledge is published and older knowledge is preserved.
* Learning connects to reflection, practice, service, mentorship, and contribution.
* Community history and cultural memory are not lost when platforms change.

### 3.2 Garvey

A healthy Garvey observes accurately, explains assessment context transparently, relies on reliable evidence, avoids permanent labels, and protects members from deterministic interpretation. Garvey should help the ecosystem understand patterns without claiming ownership over a person's destiny.

Healthy indicators include:

* Observations are evidence-based and understandable.
* Assessment outputs include context and limitations.
* Results can support growth without becoming permanent identity labels.
* Members are not punished, manipulated, or excluded because of assessment patterns.
* Garvey's role remains observation rather than command.

### 3.3 Simba

A healthy Simba interprets evidence into helpful recommendations, clear pathways, and member-centered invitations. It respects member agency and avoids manipulation, coercion, or dependency.

Healthy indicators include:

* Recommendations are explainable and mission-aligned.
* Development pathways remain optional, dignified, and understandable.
* Members can see multiple ways to grow and contribute.
* Simba strengthens other institutions rather than absorbing them.
* Guidance supports agency instead of forcing behavior.

### 3.4 Community Circles

Healthy Community Circles show participation, trust, diversity of strengths, healthy collaboration, shared learning, mutual aid, and practical contribution. Circles should convert individual growth into relational growth.

Healthy indicators include:

* Members participate consistently and meaningfully.
* Circles include different strengths, experiences, and contribution styles.
* Trust grows through practice, accountability, and shared work.
* Conflict is handled with dignity and repair where possible.
* Circles connect to Knowledge Commons, Community Operations, Marketplace, Preparedness, and mentorship.

### 3.5 Marketplace

A healthy Marketplace increases business participation, internal circulation, mutual benefit, economic resilience, and community trust. It should not become extractive or disconnected from the mission.

Healthy indicators include:

* Community businesses and members can participate with clarity.
* Value circulates within the ecosystem where appropriate.
* Marketplace activity supports real needs and ethical exchange.
* Businesses can connect to learning, operations, publishing, and investment pathways.
* Economic participation strengthens community resilience.

### 3.6 PocketPT

A healthy PocketPT strengthens member wellness, physical readiness, long-term health, consistency, and practical self-care. It should support dignity and readiness rather than shame or comparison.

Healthy indicators include:

* Members receive accessible wellness support and practical routines.
* Physical readiness connects to long-term health and preparedness.
* Health participation respects privacy, consent, and personal circumstances.
* Progress is framed as care, resilience, and capacity building.
* Wellness learning can connect to Knowledge Commons and Community Circles.

### 3.7 Publishing

A healthy Publishing institution preserves knowledge, educates members, tells community stories, documents lessons, and turns experience into shared memory. Publishing should protect dignity while making wisdom durable.

Healthy indicators include:

* Community stories, lessons, and educational materials are preserved.
* Publishing workflows respect consent, attribution, and context.
* Publications strengthen learning, culture, and intergenerational memory.
* Knowledge from pilots, operations, and institutions is documented.
* Publishing does not exploit members' stories or reduce them to marketing assets.

### 3.8 Investment Platform

A healthy Investment Platform supports ethical ownership, long-term community wealth, responsible stewardship, transparency, and patient value creation. It should connect economic growth to mission and community benefit.

Healthy indicators include:

* Opportunities are explained clearly and responsibly.
* Ownership models emphasize long-term community value.
* Investment activity avoids exploitation, hype, and opaque promises.
* Business systems, Marketplace, education, and stewardship remain connected.
* Community wealth building is treated as responsibility, not speculation.

### 3.9 STAR

A healthy STAR institution recognizes, encourages, and reinforces positive participation without turning recognition into manipulation or social ranking. STAR should celebrate contribution while preserving humility and dignity.

Healthy indicators include:

* Recognition highlights learning, service, cooperation, mentorship, and stewardship.
* Rewards encourage healthy participation without coercive gamification.
* Members understand what is being recognized and why.
* STAR supports community culture rather than status competition.
* Recognition can point members toward deeper responsibility and contribution.

### 3.10 Preparedness

A healthy Preparedness institution strengthens community resilience, resource readiness, practical coordination, emergency response, mutual aid, and local capacity. Preparedness should convert learning and cooperation into readiness.

Healthy indicators include:

* Members and Circles understand practical preparedness steps.
* Resource needs and response plans are documented where appropriate.
* Preparedness connects to Community Operations, PocketPT, Marketplace, Knowledge Commons, and Circles.
* Planning respects privacy, safety, and local context.
* Preparedness improves confidence without creating fear-driven behavior.

## 4. Institutional Review Questions

Every institution should periodically ask:

* Are we serving the mission?
* Are we serving members?
* Are we strengthening the community?
* Are we strengthening other institutions?
* Are we preserving dignity?
* Are we protecting privacy and consent?
* Are we becoming overly centralized?
* Are we transparent about our purpose, inputs, outputs, and limits?
* Are we sustainable across leadership, staffing, technology, and time?
* Are we preserving knowledge and institutional memory?
* Are our measures of success creating the right incentives?
* What is confusing, duplicated, or unnecessary?
* What should improve next?

These questions should produce recommendations, documentation updates, simplification opportunities, and institutional repair work. They should not become a hidden disciplinary process for members.

---

## 5. Architectural Boundaries

Stewardship never directly controls institutions. It is an architectural layer for visibility, alignment, and improvement.

Stewardship may:

* Observe institutional patterns.
* Measure institutional health indicators.
* Document findings, risks, dependencies, and lessons.
* Recommend improvements.
* Support institutional repair and simplification.
* Preserve institutional memory.
* Identify drift from constitutional principles.

Stewardship must not:

* Override member agency.
* Replace institutional owners.
* Centralize all decisions.
* Turn health indicators into punitive scores.
* Use private member data without consent or necessity.
* Collapse distinct institutions into one command layer.
* Convert recommendations into automatic enforcement.

The stewardship layer is healthy when it helps institutions become stronger without becoming the institution that controls all other institutions.

---

## 6. Stewardship Review Cycle

A practical stewardship rhythm may include:

1. **Institutional self-review**: The institution reviews its mission, member experience, dependencies, outputs, and risks.
2. **Evidence gathering**: Stewards gather aggregate patterns, documentation, member feedback, operational lessons, and dependency signals.
3. **Constitutional alignment check**: The institution is reviewed against mission, dignity, consent, privacy, cooperation, and long-term sustainability.
4. **Dependency review**: Stewards examine whether the institution strengthens or burdens other institutions.
5. **Improvement recommendations**: Findings are converted into prioritized recommendations.
6. **Documentation preservation**: Lessons, decisions, and open questions are recorded for future builders.
7. **Follow-up review**: The institution revisits recommendations and evaluates whether improvement occurred.

This cycle should be lightweight enough to repeat and serious enough to preserve institutional integrity.

---

## 7. Stewardship Outputs

Stewardship outputs should be documentary and advisory unless a separate governance process gives them another role.

Possible outputs include:

* Institutional health notes.
* Dependency updates.
* Risk registers.
* Improvement recommendations.
* Documentation updates.
* Simplification proposals.
* Cross-institution cooperation proposals.
* Long-term sustainability assessments.
* Lessons learned from pilots, launches, and community operations.

The most important stewardship output is institutional clarity: future builders should understand what each institution is for, how it remains healthy, and how it improves without losing its soul.

---

## 8. Long-Term Stewardship Thesis

Simba will remain healthy if its institutions can grow without losing constitutional alignment, cooperate without collapsing into one another, measure without dehumanizing members, and improve without becoming centralized control systems.

The stewardship architecture exists so the ecosystem can mature into a durable community operating system: one that future generations can understand, inherit, repair, and expand.
