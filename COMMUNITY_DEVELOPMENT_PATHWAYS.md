# Community Development Pathways

## Final Planning Document Before Implementation

### Purpose

This document defines how Simba transforms assessment intelligence into lifelong personal and community development.

Garvey identifies evidence.

Simba identifies possibilities.

This document explains how members intentionally grow.

This is a planning document only. It does not implement code, change Garvey, alter scoring, or create permanent labels.

---

## Guiding Philosophy

Garvey answers:

> Who are you today?

Simba answers:

> Who are you becoming?

Development is never finished. Every member should always have another meaningful step available.

Growth is not a label, rank, or final identity. It is a living relationship between evidence, choice, learning, practice, contribution, and reflection.

---

## Development Cycle

Every pathway should follow the same cycle:

1. **Observe** — gather signals from assessments, learning, participation, goals, and reflection.
2. **Understand** — interpret those signals without reducing the member to a fixed type.
3. **Choose** — let the member select one or more meaningful growth directions.
4. **Learn** — recommend knowledge resources that support the selected direction.
5. **Practice** — translate learning into repeatable habits, exercises, and real-world action.
6. **Contribute** — connect personal growth to family, cooperative, neighborhood, cultural, or business needs.
7. **Reflect** — help the member evaluate what changed, what still feels difficult, and what matters next.
8. **Repeat** — refresh recommendations as the member grows.

The Community Development Engine is continuous rather than linear.

## 1. Development Pathway Framework

Every member should be able to select one or more development goals. Example pathways include:

- Become a stronger leader
- Become a better communicator
- Become an entrepreneur
- Become a community organizer
- Become a mentor
- Become financially disciplined
- Become a cultural educator
- Become an agricultural steward
- Become a cooperative builder

These are goals, not permanent identities. A member may pause, change, complete, restart, or combine pathways over time.

### Pathway Structure

Every pathway should include:

- **Current evidence** — relevant assessment, learning, participation, and reflection signals.
- **Current strengths** — observed capacities the member can build from.
- **Growth opportunities** — areas where practice, guidance, or exposure may help.
- **Recommended reading** — books connected to the goal and the member's current stage.
- **Recommended audiobooks** — audio-first options for flexible learning.
- **Recommended lessons** — short guided modules or courses.
- **Recommended assessments** — optional follow-up assessments that clarify growth areas.
- **Recommended community activities** — events, circles, projects, or service opportunities.
- **Recommended mentors** — people or roles that can provide guidance.
- **Recommended projects** — practical applications that benefit the member and the community.
- **Milestones** — visible progress markers that do not become fixed labels.
- **Progress history** — completed actions, reflections, and contributions.
- **Reflection checkpoints** — intentional prompts to help members integrate learning.

### Pathway States

Future implementation may model each pathway with flexible states:

- **Exploring** — the member is considering the pathway.
- **Selected** — the member has chosen the pathway as an active goal.
- **Learning** — the member is focused on knowledge acquisition.
- **Practicing** — the member is applying skills in controlled or personal contexts.
- **Contributing** — the member is applying skills in community contexts.
- **Reflecting** — the member is reviewing progress and adjusting direction.
- **Paused** — the member has intentionally deprioritized the pathway.
- **Completed for now** — the member has reached the current milestone set and may later continue.

“Completed for now” should never mean the member is finished growing.

## 2. Pathway Lifecycle Model

The pathway lifecycle should translate the development cycle into product behavior.

### Stage 1: Pathway Discovery

Simba introduces possible pathways based on member goals, evidence, and community needs. Discovery should include both system-suggested and member-selected options.

### Stage 2: Pathway Selection

The member chooses the pathway or pathways that feel meaningful. Simba should explain why each pathway may be relevant while making clear that the member controls the choice.

### Stage 3: Baseline Context

Simba summarizes current evidence, strengths, opportunities, and relevant history. This baseline should be presented as a starting point, not a judgment.

### Stage 4: First Step Recommendation

Simba recommends one immediate next step. The first step should be small enough to start and meaningful enough to matter.

### Stage 5: Guided Learning

The member receives reading, audiobook, lesson, language, and assessment recommendations aligned with the pathway.

### Stage 6: Practice and Application

The member applies learning through exercises, habits, peer discussion, projects, or community roles.

### Stage 7: Community Contribution

Simba connects the member to contribution opportunities that match readiness, interest, and community need.

### Stage 8: Reflection and Update

The member reflects on what happened. Simba updates pathway recommendations based on completed work, contribution, new assessment evidence, and member feedback.

### Stage 9: New Meaningful Step

The pathway refreshes and offers the next meaningful step. The cycle repeats for lifelong development.

## 3. Recommendation Engine Concepts

Recommendations should change as members grow. For example:

| Timeframe | Example Leadership Recommendation |
| --- | --- |
| Month 1 | Leadership Foundations |
| Month 3 | Conflict Resolution |
| Month 6 | Cooperative Governance |
| Month 12 | Mentoring Others |

The recommendation engine should evolve with the member.

### Recommendation Principles

- **Evidence-informed, not deterministic** — evidence should guide recommendations without defining destiny.
- **Member-directed** — members should be able to accept, skip, save, or reject recommendations.
- **Community-connected** — recommendations should include personal and community benefit.
- **Stage-aware** — early pathway members need different steps than experienced contributors.
- **Load-conscious** — recommendations should respect the member's available time and capacity.
- **Transparent** — members should understand why something was suggested.
- **Refreshable** — recommendations should update when new evidence appears.

### Recommendation Categories

Future recommendations may include:

- Learning resources
- Reflection prompts
- Assessments
- Language practice
- Financial habits
- Volunteer opportunities
- Cooperative or business tasks
- Mentorship opportunities
- Peer circles
- Cultural education activities
- Project leadership roles
- Community events

### Recommendation Explanation Pattern

Every recommendation should be able to answer:

- Why this?
- Why now?
- What will I learn or practice?
- How does this benefit me?
- How does this benefit the community?
- What is the expected time commitment?
- What milestone or reflection does it support?

## 4. Milestone Framework

Milestones should show progress without creating permanent labels.

### Milestone Types

- **Learning milestones** — completed books, audiobooks, lessons, or courses.
- **Practice milestones** — repeated habits, simulations, exercises, or peer discussions.
- **Assessment milestones** — reassessments or new evidence points that clarify growth.
- **Contribution milestones** — service, project work, teaching, organizing, cooperative activity, or volunteer participation.
- **Reflection milestones** — written or spoken reflections that capture insight and intention.
- **Mentorship milestones** — receiving guidance, shadowing, mentoring others, or joining a peer circle.

### Milestone Design Rules

Milestones should:

- Recognize action, learning, and contribution.
- Avoid ranking people as better or worse.
- Avoid permanent identities.
- Be reversible or refreshable where appropriate.
- Encourage the next step rather than final status.
- Allow multiple forms of evidence.
- Be understandable to the member.

### Example Milestone Sequence: Cooperative Builder

1. Complete cooperative economics introduction.
2. Reflect on personal cooperative strengths and challenges.
3. Attend a cooperative planning session.
4. Help complete one shared task for a community project.
5. Study cooperative governance basics.
6. Participate in a decision-making circle.
7. Mentor a newer member through the introductory material.

## 5. Dashboard Journey Concepts

The future Simba dashboard should become a Development Dashboard.

Example dashboard areas:

- Current Community Archetypes
- Community Characteristics
- Growth Pathways
- Recent Progress
- Books Completed
- Audiobooks Completed
- Language Progress
- Assessments
- Community Projects
- Suggested Next Step
- Current Contribution Opportunities
- Reflection Checkpoints
- Mentor or Peer Circle Suggestions

The dashboard should always answer:

> What is my next meaningful step?

### Dashboard Journey

1. **Arrival** — the member sees the most relevant current pathway and next step.
2. **Context** — the member can review why the step was recommended.
3. **Choice** — the member can accept, save, skip, or choose a different direction.
4. **Action** — the dashboard links directly to the resource, project, activity, or reflection.
5. **Progress** — completed actions update pathway history.
6. **Reflection** — Simba asks what the member learned or noticed.
7. **Renewal** — the dashboard recommends the next meaningful step.

### Dashboard Tone

The dashboard should feel encouraging, practical, and future-oriented. It should not feel like a report card, surveillance tool, or personality label system.

## 6. Community Participation Integration

Growth should always connect back to the community.

Every pathway should answer:

- How does this growth benefit the member?
- How does this growth benefit the community?

Growth is measured by both personal development and community contribution.

### Participation Signals

Future planning may connect pathways to:

- Volunteer shifts
- Community events
- Cooperative business activities
- Study circles
- Language circles
- Mentorship sessions
- Cultural education work
- Agricultural work
- Financial literacy groups
- Conflict resolution circles
- Project teams

### Contribution Matching

Contribution opportunities should be matched by:

- Member interest
- Member readiness
- Pathway stage
- Community need
- Time commitment
- Location or remote availability
- Required skills
- Support or mentorship available

### Community Feedback

Community feedback should be handled carefully. It should support growth and reflection, not public shaming or fixed judgment. Sensitive feedback should require consent, privacy controls, and clear member-facing explanations.

## 8. Future API Considerations

This section is conceptual only and should guide later implementation planning.

Future APIs may need to support:

- Listing available pathways
- Selecting a pathway
- Pausing or resuming a pathway
- Retrieving a member's active pathways
- Retrieving current pathway evidence summaries
- Retrieving next-step recommendations
- Accepting, skipping, saving, or completing a recommendation
- Recording milestone progress
- Recording reflections
- Linking learning completions to pathways
- Linking assessment history to pathway evidence
- Linking community participation to contribution milestones
- Listing contribution opportunities

- Updating recommendation preferences

### API Guardrails

Future APIs should:

- Avoid exposing permanent labels.
- Keep assessment scoring separate from development recommendation logic.
- Preserve Garvey as the Observation Engine.
- Preserve Simba as the Development Engine.
- Provide transparent recommendation explanations.
- Respect privacy and consent around sensitive evidence.
- Allow member agency over pathway selection and recommendation response.
